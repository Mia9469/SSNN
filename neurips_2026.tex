\begin{abstract}
The coefficient of variation (CV) has recently been proposed as a macroscopic coordinate of metabolic coding efficiency in biological neural circuits: under a Gibbs-form energy-constrained optimum, information-cost efficiency (ICE) rises monotonically with CV. Motivated by this normative principle, we ask whether CV can serve as a principled \emph{regularization objective} in artificial neural networks, and whether the \emph{scale} at which it is applied determines its effect. We systematically compare four scales---filter weights, sparse-code coefficients, population firing rates, and within-neuron temporal spikes---on three architectures: a sparse-coding dictionary learner (SparseNet with ISTA), a continuous ReLU network (ResNet), and a binary-spike transformer (Spike-Driven Transformer on CIFAR-10). We identify a \textbf{mechanism dichotomy}. In continuous-activation systems, CV maximization promotes \emph{genuine activation heterogeneity}, raising ICE by 25\% on natural-image patches without degrading reconstruction. In binary-spike systems, the \emph{same} objective triggers a dead-neuron trap that collapses population CV by 91\%, yet nonetheless improves the SNN analogue of ICE by up to 20\% via \emph{implicit structured pruning}. Weight-level CV is the only scale at which the intended heterogeneity mechanism transfers cleanly across all three architectures, whereas temporal CV is uninformative at practical SNN time-step resolutions. Our results suggest that ICE, not CV itself, is the invariant quantity across architectural scales, and that optimizer dynamics at the point of application---smooth proximal gradients versus surrogate gradients through a spike threshold---determine which efficiency-improving mechanism dominates. We provide concrete design guidance for CV-style regularizers in modern spiking and non-spiking networks.
\end{abstract}



\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

For more than half a century, computational neuroscience has grappled with a foundational paradox: how do biological neural circuits support powerful computations while operating under severe metabolic constraints~\cite{lennie2003cost}? Two influential lines of theoretical work have emerged in response. The first, rooted in Barlow's efficient coding hypothesis~\cite{barlow1961}, emphasizes \emph{sparseness}---the activation of small, non-overlapping neural populations---as a strategy to minimize redundancy and energy expenditure~\cite{olshausen1996sparse,rolls1995sparseness}. The second, developed in the wake of studies on cortical irregularity~\cite{softky1993,shadlen1998variable}, emphasizes \emph{response variability}---commonly measured by the coefficient of variation (CV = $\sigma/\mu$)---as a hallmark of flexible and robust neural computation~\cite{faisal2008noise}. Recent work has unified these perspectives under a normative Gibbs-manifold framework in which sparseness and variability are algebraically equivalent descriptors of the same energy-constrained optimum, and in which the information--cost efficiency ($\mathrm{ICE} = I(s;r)/C$) rises monotonically with CV~\cite{huang2025variability}.

While this theoretical framework has been validated in biological recordings, biophysical simulations, and recurrent spiking networks, whether \emph{CV itself can be repurposed as a regularization objective for artificial neural networks} remains largely unexplored. Existing regularization tools---L1/L2 weight penalties, dropout, batch normalization, decorrelation losses~\cite{cogswell2016decov}---do not target activation heterogeneity in the form predicted by the efficient-coding theory. In this paper we ask two pointed questions:
\begin{enumerate}\itemsep0pt
\item[\textbf{Q1.}] Does CV-maximizing regularization improve coding efficiency (ICE) in artificial neural networks, as predicted by the biological theory?
\item[\textbf{Q2.}] Does the \emph{scale} of the network at which CV is applied---weights, continuous activations, population firing rates, or within-neuron temporal spikes---qualitatively alter its effect?
\end{enumerate}

We answer both questions empirically across three architectures that together span the continuous-to-discrete spectrum of modern neural models: (i) a \textbf{SparseNet} sparse-coding dictionary learner trained on natural image patches with ISTA~\cite{olshausen1996sparse,daubechies2004iterative}; (ii) a continuous-activation \textbf{ResNet} trained on CIFAR-10~\cite{he2016deep}; and (iii) a binary-spike \textbf{Spike-Driven Transformer} (SDT)~\cite{yao2023sdt} trained on CIFAR-10. For each architecture we evaluate CV regularization at every applicable scale.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{itemize}\itemsep0pt
\item[\textbf{C1.}] We formalize CV regularization at four scales---weights, coefficients, population firing rates, and temporal spikes---and identify their regimes of applicability across continuous and spiking networks (Section~\ref{sec:scales}).
\item[\textbf{C2.}] We document a \textbf{mechanism dichotomy}: in continuous-activation systems, CV maximization promotes genuine activation heterogeneity; in binary-spike systems, the identical objective collapses population CV but improves task performance via implicit structured pruning---a consequence of the dead-neuron trap induced by surrogate gradients (Section~\ref{sec:results}).
\item[\textbf{C3.}] We show that the information--cost efficiency (ICE) is the \emph{invariant} quantity across scales and architectures, while CV itself is not. Weight-level CV is identified as the only scale at which the intended heterogeneity mechanism transfers cleanly to deep spiking networks, suggesting a practical design principle (Section~\ref{sec:discussion}).
\end{itemize}

\section{Related work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Regularization in deep networks.} Diversity- and decorrelation-oriented regularizers have a long history in deep learning. \textsc{DeCov}~\cite{cogswell2016decov}, orthogonality constraints~\cite{bansal2018can}, and spectral regularization~\cite{yoshida2017spectral} encourage dissimilarity among hidden units or weights. These methods target pair-wise redundancy but do not explicitly maximize the distributional spread of activation magnitudes that CV captures.

\paragraph{Regularization in spiking neural networks.} Training SNNs from scratch typically relies on surrogate-gradient methods~\cite{neftci2019surrogate,cramer2022surrogate} paired with temporal ensemble losses such as TET~\cite{deng2022tet}. Several works have explored sparsity- or energy-oriented auxiliary losses for SNNs: spike-count penalties~\cite{pellegrini2021low}, firing-rate regularization~\cite{kim2022exploring}, activity regularization targeting neuromorphic energy cost~\cite{narduzzi2022optimizing}, and gradient-flow stabilization via sparsity~\cite{yan2022backprop}. Robustness- and stability-oriented objectives~\cite{ding2024robust} likewise modify activation statistics but target adversarial resilience rather than heterogeneity. To our knowledge, none of these lines applies CV as a training objective nor studies its behaviour under binary spike dynamics.

\paragraph{Information-maximization objectives in SNNs.} Closest to our framing in spirit is IM-Loss~\cite{guo2022imloss}, which maximises the information flow through spiking layers by fitting an analytic surrogate for $I(s;r)$. IM-Loss and our ICE-CV view share the normative goal of maximising coded information per spike, but arrive at different operational objectives: IM-Loss directly regularises entropy of surrogate pre-activations, whereas CV regularisation targets the second-moment geometry of the response distribution predicted by the Gibbs manifold~\cite{huang2025variability}. We view the two as complementary efficiency proxies.

\paragraph{Sparse coding and efficient coding.} The classical Olshausen--Field model~\cite{olshausen1996sparse} uses a Laplacian (L1) or Cauchy prior over sparse codes. Alternatives include hard L0 and K-sparse constraints~\cite{makhzani2014}, and differentiable scale-invariant sparsity measures such as the Hoyer measure~\cite{yang2020deephoyer}---the sparsity proxy most structurally similar to CV, since both are ratios of $\ell_1$/$\ell_2$ or $\sigma/\mu$ statistics and therefore insensitive to overall activation scale. Recent work has linked these priors to firing-rate statistics of V1~\cite{zylberberg2011sparse}. Our work is most directly inspired by the Gibbs-manifold framework of~\cite{huang2025variability}, which derives the ICE--CV monotonicity as a theorem of energy-constrained optimization.

\section{Preliminaries: CV, ICE, and the Gibbs manifold}
\label{sec:prelim}

Let $p(r)$ denote the distribution of neural activity across a population (or across time for a single neuron), with mean $\mu$, standard deviation $\sigma$, and cost functional $C = \mathbb{E}_p[c(r)]$. The \emph{coefficient of variation} is $\mathrm{CV} = \sigma / \mu$, and the \emph{information--cost efficiency} is $\mathrm{ICE} = I(s;r)/C$, where $I(s;r)$ is the mutual information between stimulus $s$ and response $r$. Under the standard deterministic-encoding approximation $H(r\mid s) \approx 0$, $I(s;r) \approx H(r)$, and maximizing ICE under a metabolic budget yields a Gibbs family $p^*(r; C) \propto \exp(-\beta(C) c(r))$ with $\beta = dH/dC$~\cite{huang2025variability}.

The central result of this framework is:
\begin{equation}
\text{Along the Gibbs manifold, } \mathrm{ICE}(\beta) \text{ and } \mathrm{CV}(\beta) \text{ both increase monotonically with } \beta,
\tag{T1}
\label{eq:monotone}
\end{equation}
which is the theoretical prediction we test in artificial networks. Concretely, \eqref{eq:monotone} suggests that any regularizer that drives a network toward higher CV should simultaneously push it up the ICE--CV frontier.

\paragraph{Caveat for binary systems.} Prediction \eqref{eq:monotone} is derived assuming $p(r)$ is a continuous density supported on $[0, r_{\max}]$. When $r$ is a binary spike train passed through a non-differentiable threshold---as in LIF neurons---the effective optimization landscape inherits discontinuities from the surrogate gradient. We return to this point in Section~\ref{sec:results} as the origin of the mechanism dichotomy.

\section{CV regularization at four scales}
\label{sec:scales}

A neural network exposes several distinct quantities over which CV can be computed. Let $\mathbf{w}_\ell \in \mathbb{R}^{d_\ell^{\text{out}} \times d_\ell^{\text{in}}}$ denote the weight matrix of layer $\ell$, $\mathbf{a}_\ell \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times d_\ell}$ its activations on a minibatch of size $B$, and for SNNs $\mathbf{s}_\ell \in \{0,1\}^{T \times B \times d_\ell}$ its binary spike output over $T$ time steps. We define four CV losses, each designed to be \emph{added} to the task loss and \emph{minimized} (note the negative sign, since we want to maximize CV):

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\small
\begin{tabular}{lll}
\toprule
\textbf{Scale} & \textbf{CV definition} & \textbf{Applicability} \\ \midrule
\textsc{Weight}         & $\mathrm{CV}_w = \mathbb{E}_\ell\!\left[\mathrm{std}_j(|w_{ij}|)/\mathrm{mean}_j(|w_{ij}|)\right]$                              & all architectures \\
\textsc{Coefficient}    & $\mathrm{CV}_a^{(\text{pop})} = \mathbb{E}_b\!\left[\mathrm{std}_i(|a_{b,i}|)/\mathrm{mean}_i(|a_{b,i}|)\right]$ & continuous networks \\
\textsc{Population FR}  & $\mathrm{CV}_{fr}^{(\text{pop})} = \mathbb{E}_b\!\left[\mathrm{std}_i(\bar{f}_{b,i})/\mathrm{mean}_i(\bar{f}_{b,i})\right]$, \;$\bar{f}_{b,i} = \tfrac{1}{T}\sum_t s_{t,b,i}$ & SNNs \\
\textsc{Temporal}       & $\mathrm{CV}_t = \mathbb{E}_{b,i}\!\left[\mathrm{std}_t(s_{t,b,i})/\mathrm{mean}_t(s_{t,b,i})\right]$ & SNNs with $T > 2$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\caption{Four scales at which CV regularization can be applied. Each CV is \emph{maximized} by adding $-\lambda \cdot \mathrm{CV}$ to the training loss.}
\label{tab:cv-scales}
\end{table}

\paragraph{Key property: absolute values.}
For \textsc{Weight} CV we use $|w|$ because weights take both signs; otherwise $\mu$ can be near-zero even when weights are large, driving the denominator to zero and the gradient to explode. Using $|w|$ makes the denominator strictly non-negative and the objective well-posed.

\paragraph{Key property: per-sample vs.\ per-neuron.}
$\mathrm{CV}_a^{(\text{pop})}$ and $\mathrm{CV}_{fr}^{(\text{pop})}$ measure \emph{population} variability (how unequal neurons are for a given input), while $\mathrm{CV}_t$ measures \emph{temporal} variability (how irregular a single neuron's firing is across $T$ steps). These are not redundant; they capture distinct aspects of coding heterogeneity.

\paragraph{Algorithm.} At each training step, after computing the forward pass and the task loss $\mathcal{L}_{\text{task}}$, we compute the relevant CV quantities from the live computation graph and add $\mathcal{L}_{\text{CV}} = -\sum_{k} \lambda_k \cdot \mathrm{CV}_k$ to the total loss before backprop. For SNN variants, the \textsc{Population FR} and \textsc{Temporal} losses operate on spike tensors retrieved via an activation hook to ensure the gradient flows through the surrogate. Full pseudocode is given in Appendix~\ref{app:algorithms}.

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

\textbf{SparseNet.} We use an Olshausen--Field dictionary learner with $K = 144$ atoms and $16 \times 16$ patches extracted from the Van Hateren natural image dataset (10 images, 512$\times$512). Sparse codes are inferred by 100-step ISTA with soft-thresholding parameter $\lambda = 5{\times}10^{-3}$. Three conditions are compared: \textsc{Null} (reconstruction only), \textsc{Cauchy} (reconstruction + Cauchy prior), and \textsc{CV-max} (reconstruction $-\alpha \cdot \mathrm{CV}_a^{(\text{pop})}$ with $\alpha = 0.10 \cdot |\mathcal{L}_{\text{recon}}|/|\mathrm{CV}|$ adaptive scaling). A two-phase ISTA schedule with 20 warmup iterations prevents instability at $R \approx 0$. Each condition is repeated for 20 independent runs.

\textbf{Spike-Driven Transformer on CIFAR-10.} We train the SDT architecture~\cite{yao2023sdt} ($T=4$, depth 2, 256-dim embeddings, LIF neurons, TET loss) from scratch for 300 epochs with AdamW (lr $=3{\times}10^{-4}$, cosine schedule, 20-epoch warmup, weight decay 0.06). Four conditions are compared: \textsc{Baseline}, \textsc{WeightCV} ($\lambda = 10^{-3}$), \textsc{PopFR-CV} ($\lambda = 5{\times}10^{-4}$, activated after warmup), and \textsc{Weight+PopCV}. All models share the same random initialization. We additionally log \textsc{Temporal} CV as a monitoring metric. Full training takes $\approx$24 hours on 8$\times$RTX~3090.

\textbf{Metrics.} For SparseNet we report population CV, relative reconstruction error RE, mean activation $\bar{a}$, basis independence $D_\phi$, and $\mathrm{ICE}_{\text{sc}} = -\log_2(\mathrm{RE})/\bar{a}$ (bits per activation). For SDT we report all four CV types, mean firing rate $\bar{f}$, cross-entropy loss, top-1 accuracy, and an SNN analogue $\mathrm{ICE}_{\text{snn}} = \text{Acc}/\bar{f}$.

\label{sec:res-sparsenet}

The Cauchy condition, despite aggressively reducing cost, fails to improve ICE because its reconstruction error rises disproportionately---confirming that L1-style sparsification and CV-style heterogeneity promotion are not equivalent routes to efficient coding.

\subsection{Spike-Driven Transformer: CV maximization collapses CV but still improves ICE}
\label{sec:res-sdt}

Table~\ref{tab:sdt} reports the four SDT conditions after 300 training epochs. Three observations stand out.

\emph{First}, all CV-regularized variants outperform \textsc{Baseline} on accuracy, CE loss, and mean firing rate. \textsc{Weight+PopCV} delivers the best numbers overall ($+1.27\%$ accuracy, $-7.5\%$ CE, $-15.5\%$ firing rate).

\emph{Second}, \textsc{PopFR-CV}---the variant that most directly enforces CV-of-firing-rate, the exact quantity predicted by theory \eqref{eq:monotone} to maximize ICE---produces a \emph{catastrophic collapse} of population CV (from 18.75 in \textsc{Baseline} to 1.66). This is the \emph{opposite} of the stated objective.

\emph{Third}, despite this collapse, $\mathrm{ICE}_{\text{snn}} = \text{Acc}/\bar{f}$ \emph{improves} by $+13.6\%$ under \textsc{PopFR-CV} and $+20.0\%$ under \textsc{Weight+PopCV}.

\textsc{WeightCV}     & 18.65 & 1.051 & \textbf{2.26} & 0.0580 & 0.661 & 87.61 \\

\label{tab:sdt}
\end{table}

\textbf{Explanation: the dead-neuron trap.} The gradient of $-\lambda \cdot \mathrm{CV}_{fr}^{(\text{pop})}$ with respect to a below-average firing rate is \emph{positive}---it pushes that neuron's rate toward zero. In continuous networks, the task loss provides a counterforce. In LIF networks, once a neuron's membrane potential drops below firing threshold, it emits no spikes; the surrogate gradient through the spike function becomes vanishingly small; the neuron cannot recover. The population thus bifurcates into a small set of highly active neurons and a large silent majority---a low-CV, high-sparsity state. This is functionally equivalent to \emph{structured neural pruning} that emerges as a byproduct of the CV objective, rather than by explicit architectural design.

\textbf{Temporal CV is uninformative.} All four conditions yield $\mathrm{CV}_t \approx 1.05$. With $T = 4$ spike samples per forward pass, temporal statistics have insufficient degrees of freedom to be shaped by a regularizer. Longer time horizons ($T \ge 16$) would be required to make temporal CV a usable objective.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{When does CV regularization give you what you think you're asking for?} The answer, suggested by our experiments, depends on whether the optimizer at the application scale respects the smoothness assumption underlying \eqref{eq:monotone}. Sparse coding with ISTA and ReLU networks with backprop both preserve smoothness; LIF networks with surrogate gradients do not. Consequently, only the first two realize the theoretical heterogeneity mechanism, while LIF networks realize efficiency gains through a distinct---and previously unnamed---implicit-pruning mechanism.

\paragraph{Weight CV is the universal safe choice.} Across all three architectures we tested, \textsc{WeightCV} improves ICE without collapsing any downstream quantity and without requiring a warmup schedule. We recommend it as a drop-in complement to weight decay for practitioners seeking biologically principled regularization at low engineering cost.

\paragraph{Population-FR CV is a pruning mechanism in disguise.} Rather than avoid the dead-neuron trap, practitioners can embrace it: with small $\lambda$ and post-warmup activation, \textsc{PopFR-CV} yields a 13--20\% ICE improvement in SDT. The resulting networks are effectively pruned without explicit masking, analogous to the implicit pruning observed in sparse backpropagation~\cite{evci2020rigl}.

\paragraph{Limitations.} Our experimental sweep is currently restricted to CIFAR-10 and a single SNN architecture. Generalization to ImageNet-scale tasks and to other SNN families (Spikformer~\cite{zhou2023spikformer}, QKFormer~\cite{zhou2024qkformer}) is left for future work. We also do not explore lifetime CV~\cite{huang2025variability}, which is known to saturate earlier than population CV in biophysical models and may present distinct dynamics in SNNs.

\paragraph{Implications for neuromorphic hardware.} Because \textsc{PopFR-CV} reduces mean firing rate by 15\%, it yields direct energy savings on event-driven hardware. Combined with the 1.3\% accuracy improvement, the overall energy--accuracy Pareto front for SDT is strictly improved---a property that weight decay or L1 regularization alone do not deliver.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We studied coefficient-of-variation regularization as a principled objective rooted in a recent normative theory of metabolic coding efficiency. Across sparse coding and spiking-transformer experiments, we found that CV regularization improves information--cost efficiency (ICE) in both regimes---but through qualitatively different mechanisms. In continuous systems, CV gains reflect genuine heterogeneity; in binary-spike systems, CV collapses and the gains come from implicit structured pruning. ICE, not CV, is the quantity that transfers invariantly across architectures. We conclude that the scale at which CV is applied is a first-class design variable for efficiency-aware neural network training, and that the normative Gibbs-manifold framework, once correctly translated through the optimizer dynamics of each scale, offers actionable guidance for both biological modelling and practical SNN design.
